    Complexity is often irreducible

    Complexity is often irreducible unless you change the problem #

    We work in a pretty complex industry. Understanding a single customer, and every aspect of what they’re doing, is pretty hard. Extending it to lots of customers is even harder. Still, people try, but I see a logical error over and over again when they do so.

    Make me a song #

    Let’s pick a different industry just to use as an analogy - music.

    If I were to analyze all popular songs written in the last 50 years, I’d notice a few significant trends.

    One famous trend is that all pop songs use “the same chords”. To a first approximation, that’s true.

    Most pop choruses will involve the same set of dominant, minor sixth, prefect fourth, and perfect fifth chords, because that’s what sounds good to a (mostly American) ear.

    A naive person might think, “okay, since most pop songs have four chords, I’m going to make software that helps people write songs. Here’s how it works - we give you a list of chords. You pick one, and it suggests the next chords you probably want, and in the end the song is written!”

    Well, one problem is Jimmy will get laughed out of the room.

    Music isn’t made that way. For one, if you were reading carefully above, we described our chord trend as an approximation. That’s because any particular pop song has all sorts of extra stuff, like diminished 7ths to make the transition to the perfect fifth sound better, or a bridge that uses a minor second, or a key change at the emotional climax of the song.

    And that’s just in the chords! Even the most generic pop song is really complicated - it has the drums coming in on a certain beat, and the bass line fades in a very particular way at 59 seconds in, and it has harmonies by Rihanna over the top, and it’s mastered so that the various sounds in the track are balanced.

    Once you understand all the complexity that goes into making music, you wonder: How could anyone incorporate all of this just to make a 3 minute track?

    But however they do it, if you want them to use your software, they’ll want to have much of the control and flexbility that they had in whatever they were using before.

    When you’re recording music using a gramaphone, you’re not “just choosing chords”. You get a lot of control over the individual notes played, how loud they are, how much vibrato you use, etc. And so any software you build for “making a pop song with Microsoft Clippy” has to emulate that complexity - it has to let the user do all of the things the care about from the old process in the new one.

    Of course, your software doesn’t have to do absolutely everything. It used to be super annoying to loop tracks manually, until digital loopers were invented. Most pop artists can now lay in a simple beat via a digital trapset, rather than needing to hire a drummer to record one. So software can help make things better.

    But the end goal, what the artist is trying to do, that’s often as complex (or more complex) than what they used to make. Yes, even for the pop songs.

    Your job is to make that easier, but you don’t get to change what they’re making, you have to change what you’re making.

    Changing the problem #

    The most common behavior I see is saying that you’re solving the problem of making a pop song, but the problem you’re actually solving is a tool for smushing a bunch of reasonable sounding chords together. Those are not the same problem!

    They are similar in some sense, but there’s a bunch of steps between one and the other, and no one bothers to talk about those steps. And yet, when asked about some of the subtlety and complexity that hasn’t been addressed yet, I receive the same bromides about how most songs just have four chords. I know that! Everyone knows that!

    I’m reminded of the trope about how to draw an owl, which starts with two circles, and then instructs the student to “draw the rest of the owl”.

    Basically every problem that anyone tries to solve has an 80 / 20 where the 80 looks relatively simple and straightforward and extendable. 80% of companies in this category do X. 80% of configurations look like Y. 80% of analyses look like Z.

    But you can’t look at the ever-present 80 / 20 and then draw the conclusion that the 20 is unnecessary or unimportant because it’s possible you’ve taken a path down the first 80% that is unrecoverable. You might be in a dead-end and unable to satisfy the other 20% of requirements.

    And you’ll have built the software equivalent of a toilet that only lets you expel 80% of a bowel movement. The number of people using it won’t be 80%…it’ll be 0%.

    Pick something smaller #

    The key is to understand exactly what the 80 / 20 refers to. Is it that 80% of customers have one piece of the puzzle that looks the same? That’s nothing - those are everywhere.

    What you want is to find 80% of instances of a problem that are solved end-to-end with the same, simple solution.

    To take our music example, instead of solving the problem of “make a pop song”, our problem could instead be “let me select multiple tracks and layer them over each other”. That’s a very specific problem that most but not all artists have and it’s theoretically a simple software solution.

    It’s a way less sexy problem, though. The addressable market is smaller, and the amount we can charge customers is probably less. But we’d have some confidence in solving something end-to-end.

    Meanwhile our chord smusher basically solved no problem by itself. It’s not even on the path to solving something end-to-end.

    I think in most cases, leaders at our organization (and most others) would be best served by picking less ambitious problems. Not because we shouldn’t try to do hard things, but because the ambition of the problem can mask a gap in structured thinking about the solution. We’d be better served by focusing on tractable, specific things we can solve, rather than intractable, wide-ranging, longer timespan projects where we can get to 80% soon-ish, with the rest undefined and many years away.

    Make a better widget, connect it to the rest of the machine #

    There are ways of taking your unsexy thing and making it better without overselling. For example, if your solution solves a critical sub-problem but isn’t end-to-end alone, let the customer use an escape hatch to solve the rest of it in another tool. If they get end-to-end faster, you win, even if they are using your competitor to get the last mile done.

    The main gap I see from decision-makers is not ignorance of the logic above, but the hesitation to apply it. It’s hard to acknowledge that your product will only get the customer part of the way.

    Most internal company incentives are to talk about the ideal. And because decision-makers don’t always have visibility into why we’re doing the things we’re doing, it’s your job to distinguish between the bad 80% problem (80% of lots of things but 100% of nothing) and the good 80% (100% of a small problem end to end), and make sure you’re working on the latter.

    If you just sell the most interesting problem everywhere, it might work for a while, but in 9 or 12 months people are going to realize that your thing didn’t actually create value for customers. Steering into transparency about problem complexity, and the (initial) inadequacy of the solution you’re hoping to build, will allow real decisions about the value you’re creating.
